# The Skills Map
## The commentary layer behind the /route command

This file is the *why*. It does not route you, and it deliberately does not
carry the routing table. The operational "this task goes to that method"
lookup lives in the `/route` slash command in User Preferences, and that is
its single source of truth. If you ever find a task-to-method table creeping
into this file, delete it. The day two copies exist, one of them starts lying.

So think of it this way. `/route` is the mechanic who fixes the car. This file
is the manual you read on a slow afternoon to understand what each tool in the
box actually does, where it came from, and how to get better at swinging it.

---

## How to use this file

Three jobs, and only three:

1. **Understand a tool.** You got routed to Annie Duke and want to know what
   "think in bets" really means and why it works. Read her entry.
2. **Get better at a tool.** Each entry ends with a "how to practice" block.
   That is the part most skill catalogs skip, and it is the part that actually
   moves you.
3. **Sharpen the meta-skill.** The last two sections are about the routing
   itself: how to get faster at diagnosing which tool a task wants, which is
   the highest-leverage skill in the whole document.

What this file is *not*: a place to look up which method fits which task. That
is `/route`'s job. Type the command and let it route.

## The one idea everything hangs on

Here is a finding that should bother you a little.

Philip Tetlock, a psychologist at Penn, ran the longest study of expert
prediction ever attempted. Decades. Thousands of forecasts from people paid to
know things: economists, intelligence analysts, pundits. Then he checked who
was actually right.

The losers had something in common. Tetlock called them hedgehogs, borrowing
an old line: the fox knows many things, the hedgehog knows one big thing. The
hedgehogs had a single grand theory and jammed every problem into it. Markets
are everything. Or psychology is everything. Or game theory is everything. One
hammer, and the whole world conveniently full of nails.

The foxes won. They carried a bag of small tools and switched between them
based on what the problem in front of them actually was.

That is the entire premise of `/route`, and the entire premise of this file.
There is no super-skill. Grit will not fix a retention problem. A checklist
will not make you brave in a salary negotiation. Each tool below is sharp for
*one kind* of problem and dull for the rest. The skill that ties them together
is not any of them. It is the diagnosis: naming what kind of problem you are
holding before you reach for anything.

Get that one habit and the toolbox starts working. Skip it and you are a
hedgehog with a very nice collection of hammers.

## The tools

### 1. Getting good at something — deliberate practice

Most people believe practice makes perfect. It does not. *The right kind* of
practice does, and the difference is enormous.

Anders Ericsson spent thirty years studying violinists, chess masters, and
memory champions, and his finding was inconvenient: the elite performers were
not grinding more hours of comfortable repetition. They were doing *deliberate
practice*. Focused work, at the edge of their ability, on the specific thing
they were worst at, with immediate feedback. Not the scales they could already
play. The passage they kept fumbling.

Two researchers stack on top of him. Robert Bjork, a UCLA memory scientist,
found that the conditions that make learning feel *easy* (re-reading, watching
a tutorial again) make it stick *less*, and the ones that feel hard (pulling
the answer from memory, spacing practice across days) make it stick *more*. He
calls the good kind "desirable difficulties." And Richard Feynman, the
physicist, left behind the simplest test of real understanding: if you cannot
explain it out loud, in plain words, you have memorized a shape, not learned a
thing.

When this fits: anytime the goal is mastery and your practice is not
converting into ability. The tell is "I keep studying but it does not stick."

How to practice it:
- Close the book. Practice by pulling answers out of an empty head, not by
  re-reading. Retrieval beats recognition every time.
- Space it. Hit a topic, then return to it on day 3, day 7, day 14. The slight
  pain of having half-forgotten it is the mechanism working.
- Attack your weak spots on purpose, not the parts you enjoy because you are
  already good at them.
- Use the Feynman test as a gap-finder. Explain it aloud. Where you stall is
  exactly where to drill next.

### 2. Doing it consistently — systems and if-then plans

You do not have a motivation problem. You have a missing sentence.

Peter Gollwitzer, an NYU psychologist, spent his career on one boring question:
why do good intentions die? His answer, backed by a pile of studies, is that
vague intentions ("I will exercise more") barely move behavior, but a specific
*if-then* plan ("when I finish my morning coffee, I will do twenty pushups")
roughly doubles or triples follow-through. The trick is that you pre-decide, so
tomorrow-you does not get to negotiate.

James Clear packages the rest of it. His line from *Atomic Habits*: you do not
rise to the level of your goals, you fall to the level of your systems. Design
the environment so the good thing is obvious and easy and the bad thing is
invisible and hard. And his two-minute rule: if a habit will not start, the
version you committed to is too big. Shrink it until starting is almost
embarrassing.

When this fits: you know exactly what to do and keep not doing it. The gap is
between deciding and acting, not between not-knowing and knowing.

How to practice it:
- Write the if-then sentence literally. "After I [existing reliable habit], I
  will [tiny version of the new thing]."
- Shrink the action until it is laughable, then do the laughable version. Motion
  first, heroics later.
- Engineer the environment the night before. Every click or step you remove in
  advance is one that cannot become tomorrow's excuse.
- Never miss twice. One miss is an accident. Two is the start of a new habit.

### 3. Deciding with bad information — think in bets

Annie Duke played poker professionally for twenty years before she wrote about
decisions, which turns out to be the perfect training. Poker forces you to act
on incomplete information and then live with random outcomes.

Her core move: stop "resulting." A good decision can produce a bad outcome and
a bad decision can get lucky, so judge the *quality of the bet*, not the result
it happened to land. The question is never "am I right?" It is "what is the
bet, and what are the odds?"

Daniel Kahneman, the only psychologist to win the Nobel in economics, hands you
the brake. Your fast, intuitive brain is confident and frequently wrong, so
before you commit, anchor on the *base rate* (how often does this kind of thing
work out for people in general?) and run a *premortem*: imagine it is six
months later and the decision failed, then ask out loud why.

When this fits: a real fork where you cannot know the answer in advance.
Offer A versus offer B. Commit now versus wait.

How to practice it:
- Before any real decision, write the base rate first. Not your gut. The
  outside-view number.
- Run the premortem out loud. The failure story you tell yourself in advance is
  usually the one you would otherwise walk straight into.
- Separate the decision from the outcome when you review. Ask "was that a good
  bet given what I knew," not "did it work out."

### 4. Betting without blowing up — the barbell

Nassim Taleb was a derivatives trader before he became a writer, and he spent
that career thinking about one thing: how to survive being wrong.

His move is the *barbell*. Put most of your resources somewhere boringly safe,
and a small slice into wild, asymmetric shots where the downside is capped (you
can only lose the slice) but the upside is open-ended. Avoid the mushy middle,
the bets that feel sensible but can quietly take you down. And his iron rule:
never, ever take a bet that can wipe you out, no matter how good the odds look.
Because you only have to be wiped out once.

When this fits: any move with real downside and a fat tail. A career pivot, a
big financial commitment, a venture. Especially when the optimistic case is
loud and the ruin case is being waved away.

How to practice it:
- For any big move, write the worst case in one sentence. If the worst case is
  "I am wiped out," do not take the bet however attractive it looks.
- Cap the downside on purpose. What is the most this can cost me, and can I
  survive that? If yes, the upside can be as wild as you like.
- Prefer many small capped bets over one large uncapped one.

### 5. Building rare leverage — deep work and career capital

Cal Newport, a computer scientist who also writes about work, has an unpopular
opinion: "follow your passion" is bad advice. The thing that makes work
fulfilling and well-paid is not pre-existing passion. It is *career capital*,
rare and valuable skills you build by getting so good they cannot ignore you.
And you build those through *deep work*: long, undistracted, cognitively
demanding stretches, which is exactly the kind of work a buzzing phone destroys.

Reid Hoffman, who co-founded LinkedIn, adds the portfolio view. You are a
start-up: run an ABZ plan (Plan A, a fallback Plan B, and a worst-case Plan Z
you can survive on), and treat your network as a real asset rather than
something you panic-build when you need a job.

When this fits: the goal is leverage in a market. Getting hired, getting good
enough to get hired, building a skill that compounds.

How to practice it:
- Schedule deep-work blocks and defend them like meetings. Phone in another
  room, not face-down on the desk.
- Pick the one skill that would most raise your market value and pour the deep
  blocks into it. Capital, not dabbling.
- Treat the network as ongoing maintenance, a few real conversations a month,
  not an emergency you spin up when desperate.

### 6. Getting to yes — tactical empathy and influence

Chris Voss was the FBI's lead international kidnapping negotiator, which makes
a salary call sound like a spa day. His method, *tactical empathy*, works
because it takes the pressure off you and puts the thinking back on the other
side. Calibrated "how" and "what" questions ("how am I supposed to do that?")
hand them your problem to solve. Mirroring and labeling ("it sounds like timing
is the real concern") make people feel understood, which is what actually opens
them up. And his most useful trick for anyone who flinches: after you state your
number, *stop talking*. Silence does the work. People who undersell themselves
cannot survive the silence and start cutting their own price.

Robert Cialdini, the influence researcher, gives you the six levers people
respond to: reciprocity, commitment and consistency, social proof, authority,
liking, and scarcity. Useful both to deploy and to notice when they are being
used on you.

When this fits: any room where someone else has something you want and you have
to move them. Negotiation, persuasion, a hard ask.

How to practice it:
- Replace "okay, I could do less" with a calibrated question: "how can we get
  to [your number]?" Now it is a shared problem.
- After you state a number or a position, count to five in your head and say
  nothing. Rehearse this, because the instinct to fill silence is strong.
- Label the awkward thing out loud before they do. Naming it drains it.

### 7. Keeping going when it is hard — grit, mindset, control, small steps

This is four tools for one job, because "it is hard and I want to stop" has
several flavors and they need different medicine.

Angela Duckworth studied who survives brutal training and who quits, and the
predictor was not talent. It was *grit*: passion plus perseverance toward a
long goal. Carol Dweck found that people with a *growth mindset* (ability is
built, not fixed) treat a failure as data about the approach, while people with
a fixed mindset treat it as a verdict on themselves and quit. The Stoics,
across two thousand years, kept returning to one move: spend your energy only on
what you control, and let the rest go. And Anne Lamott, writing about writing,
gave us the cure for paralysis. Her brother had a giant school report on birds
due, untouched, and was frozen by the size of it. Their father said: "Bird by
bird, buddy. Just take it bird by bird." Perfectionism freezes you; the next
small piece unfreezes you.

When this fits: the blocker is not knowledge or method. It is staying in the
chair. Long grind, repeated rejection, a goal far enough away to feel
hopeless.

How to practice it:
- When a setback hits, write one sentence: "what does this tell me about the
  approach?" That is the growth-mindset move, and it converts a gut-punch into
  information.
- Sort the situation into "in my control" and "not in my control," then put your
  energy entirely in the first column. The job market is not in your control.
  The number of applications you send is.
- When the task feels too big to start, name the single next small piece and do
  only that. Bird by bird.

### 8. Not screwing up a complex procedure — the checklist

Atul Gawande is a surgeon, and he discovered something almost insulting in its
simplicity. A short checklist, the kind pilots use, cut surgical complications
and deaths dramatically across hospitals worldwide. Not because surgeons forgot
how to operate. Because under pressure, in complexity, smart and experienced
people skip steps. The checklist catches the skip.

When this fits: a process with several steps where missing one is expensive and
the cost of a miss is high. Visa filings. Application packages. Anything you
will do under time pressure where "I forgot to include X" is a real failure
mode.

How to practice it:
- Build the checklist once, when you are calm, for any procedure you will repeat
  or any high-stakes submission. Then actually run it, out loud, at the moment.
- Keep it short. A checklist that lists every obvious step gets ignored. List
  the steps that get *skipped*, the killer items.

### 9. The locked door — resourcefulness

Sometimes the official path is slow, blocked, or simply not built for someone
in your position. This is the home of your Madiskarte family: the opportunist
who spots the gap, the bricoleur who builds from what is already lying around,
the maverick who takes the legal-but-unconventional move most people skip.

The shared move is a reframe: treat the constraint as a design parameter, not a
stop sign. "Canadian experience required" looks like a locked door until you
ask what is actually behind the phrase, inventory what you already have that
could satisfy it, and find the side entrances (a referral that skips the filter,
a remote contract that manufactures the experience, employers who hire
newcomers on purpose).

When this fits: the front door is closed and the question is "how do I get
around this," not "what is the correct procedure."

How to practice it:
- Before accepting any "requirement," ask: is this a hard wall or a lazy proxy?
  What is the employer or system *actually* worried about, and can I answer that
  another way?
- Inventory before you acquire. What status, document, relationship, or prior
  experience do you already hold that could be repurposed here?
- Keep it legal and keep the risks visible. Resourcefulness is finding the real
  door, not pretending walls are not there.

### 10. Making it clear — clarity discipline

This one layers under all the others. Whatever you are doing, if the output is
words, it has to be clear, and clarity is a discipline, not a gift.

Strunk and White gave the mechanical rules: omit needless words, prefer the
active voice, make every word tell. William Zinsser, in *On Writing Well*,
added the human layer: cut the clutter, but keep the warmth and the rhythm, so
the prose sounds like a person and not a form.

When this fits: any time you are writing something that has to land. A cover
letter, a personal statement, an outreach message, an essay.

How to practice it:
- Write the draft, then cut every word that is not doing work. Read it again. Cut
  more. The second cut is where the clarity is.
- Read it aloud. Your ear catches the clutter and the stumbles your eye skips.

## The meta-skill: getting faster at the diagnosis

Here is the part that matters most, and it is the part nobody trains.

Owning ten tools is not the win. A hedgehog with a full toolbox is still a
hedgehog if he reaches for the same one every time. The win is the *diagnosis*:
naming what kind of problem you are holding, fast, before you reach.

The good news is that this is a trainable skill, and the drill is almost
absurdly simple. Before you solve anything, say out loud (or write) one
sentence: "this is a [kind] problem." Skill acquisition. Consistency. Decision
under uncertainty. Locked door. Persistence. Then check it against the routing
categories `/route` uses. That is it. That is the rep.

Do it on real problems for a few weeks and something shifts. You stop
defaulting to your favorite tool. You start seeing that "I keep not sending
applications" is a consistency problem, not a motivation failure, and that "I
freeze in negotiations" is a tactics-or-belief fork, not a personality flaw.
The diagnosis becomes automatic, which is exactly what expertise feels like
from the inside.

And the beautiful part: you do not have to do the routing by hand. You built a
machine for it. Type `/route` in front of the problem and watch it diagnose,
then study how it landed there. Over time the machine teaches you the move, and
eventually you can do it without the machine. That is the whole point of a
deliberate-practice loop: the scaffolding comes off.

How to practice the meta-skill:
- For one week, prefix your real problems with the diagnosis sentence before
  doing anything else. Just the naming.
- Run the harder ones through `/route` and compare: did you diagnose the same
  category it did? Where you disagreed is your learning edge.
- Notice your default. Everyone has a favorite tool they over-apply. Catch
  yourself reaching for it on a problem it does not fit.

## How this connects to the PR goal

Most of the road to permanent residency is routable, which is the quiet reason
this toolbox matters for you specifically. A rough map:

- Getting job-ready in a tech skill is a **deliberate-practice** problem
  (entry 1), not a "watch more tutorials" problem.
- Sending applications consistently is a **systems** problem (entry 2), not a
  willpower one.
- Choosing a PR pathway is a **decision under uncertainty** (entry 3), and a
  high-stakes one, so it pulls in verification and live data alongside the
  method.
- A big irreversible immigration bet wants the **barbell** lens (entry 4): what
  is the ruin case, and is it capped?
- The job hunt itself is a **career-capital and network** problem (entry 5).
- Salary and offer conversations are **negotiation** (entry 6).
- The long wait, the rejections, the delays are a **persistence** problem
  (entry 7), and which of the four sub-tools you need depends on whether you are
  facing a mindset issue, a control issue, or simple paralysis.
- Filing anything official is a **checklist** problem (entry 8).
- "Canadian experience" and similar walls are **locked-door** problems
  (entry 9).
- Every essay, statement, and outreach message is a **clarity** problem
  (entry 10).

The single highest-leverage skill across all of it is still the diagnosis:
knowing which of these you are actually holding on any given day. That is what
`/route` runs, and that is what this file is here to help you internalize.
